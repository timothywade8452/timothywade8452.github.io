First, what is “reta”?

The word behind all the searches.

“Reta” is the nickname people use for retatrutide — an investigational weight-loss injection from Eli Lilly that hits three hunger hormones at once (GLP-1, GIP and glucagon). In its 2026 trial results it produced some of the largest weight loss ever recorded for a medicine, which is why it's suddenly everywhere: TikTok, Reddit, the news.

Here's the catch almost nobody says out loud: retatrutide is not approved and you cannot legally buy it yet. A prescription launch isn't expected until around 2028. That gap — huge demand, zero availability — is exactly why so many “reta” products have appeared online overnight.

The honest bottom line: if you're searching for a “reta pill” you can buy today, you're not looking for the drug (you can't get it) — you're looking for an over-the-counter option. That's the category RetaFit sits in.

“Reta pills” vs the injection — the part that matters

Because reta pills or injection and do reta pills work are two of the most-searched questions right now.

Retatrutide is a peptide. Peptides swallowed as a pill are mostly destroyed by stomach acid before they reach your blood — that's the whole reason the real drug is injected. So the “reta pills” and “oral retatrutide” listings floating around peptide shops are, at best, inconsistent and, at worst, unregulated grey-market products.

RetaFit doesn't pretend to be oral retatrutide. It's a normal dietary supplement — a daily capsule built around metabolism ingredients like green tea extract, guarana and N-acetyl-L-carnitine. It won't behave like a prescription injection, and any advert claiming otherwise should be a red flag.
